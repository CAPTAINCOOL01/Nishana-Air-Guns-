Licence-exempt airguns: the precise rule.

Nishana sells one airgun: the .177/4.5 mm, approximately 3-joule Star RX Gen 3. That model is licence-exempt—not proof that every airgun in India can be owned without a licence.

Answer first

Nishana sells one licence-exempt airgun in India: the .177 inch/4.5 mm, approximately 3-joule Star RX Gen 3. The Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA) explains the opposite side of the line clearly: an air weapon with muzzle energy exceeding 20 J and/or a bore exceeding .177 inch/4.5 mm is subject to licensing. A buyer must check both calibre and energy; “airgun” by itself never proves exemption.

The Arms Rules 2016 distinction

Schedule I, category III(f) of the Arms Rules, 2016 divides air weapons by muzzle energy and bore. The MHA’s public FAQ summarizes the licensed category as air rifles and air guns exceeding 20 joules (15 ft-lb) and/or exceeding .177 inch (4.5 mm). “And/or” matters: exceeding either limit can move an air weapon into the licensed category.

The useful buyer test is therefore two-part. Confirm that the bore is no greater than .177 inch/4.5 mm, and confirm that the model’s documented muzzle energy is not above 20 J. Nishana’s Star RX Gen 3 listing documents a .177-calibre, approximately 3-joule configuration within both limits. That avoids the common but unsafe shortcut of deciding from calibre, velocity or power source alone.

What “licence-exempt” actually means

Licence-exempt describes the qualifying air weapon’s treatment for the buyer’s individual arms-licensing requirement. It does not mean the product is outside all regulation, that anyone may sell it informally, or that the transaction can be anonymous.

The MHA FAQ on the Arms Rules 2016 says category III(f)(ii) air weapons are not subjected to individual licensing requirements, but may be kept and sold only through air-weapon dealers holding a Form VIII-A licence. Rule 84 also requires the seller to obtain the buyer’s identification and residence proof and issue an invoice containing those particulars at sale.

Form VIII-A is the dealer’s licence, not an arms licence the buyer applies for. Expect the sales process to request both identity and residence proof. Ask which documents are accepted rather than assuming one document satisfies both.

Nishana’s stocking policy

Nishana’s rule is narrower and easier to audit than a broad “airguns need no licence” slogan: sell only the .177/4.5 mm, approximately 3-joule Star RX Gen 3. More generally, a spring-piston, CO₂ or PCP design does not become licence-exempt merely because of how it is powered; documented energy and bore are what matter for this distinction.

The Star RX Gen 3 product page identifies its calibre and publishes its energy specification. Those same two checks should be visible for any airgun considered elsewhere. A velocity figure in feet per second is not a substitute for muzzle energy: joules depend on projectile mass as well as speed.

The policy does not determine the status of products sold elsewhere; buyers should check those airguns against the official rules and local circumstances.

The compliant buyer journey

  1. Check both specifications. Look for .177 inch/4.5 mm bore and documented energy at or below 20 J. Do not infer energy from appearance, marketing language or mechanism.
  2. Buy through the correct dealer channel. The MHA FAQ assigns licence-exempt air weapons to air-weapon dealers holding Form VIII-A.
  3. Provide identity and residence proof. These are required for the sale even though the qualifying air weapon is licence-exempt for the buyer.
  4. Receive and retain the invoice. Rule 84 requires an invoice containing the buyer particulars. Check that the product description and your details are accurate.
  5. Recheck location-specific rules. Before transport or use, ask the appropriate local authority about any current order or restriction that may affect your area.

An online checkout does not erase these steps. The sales channel changes neither the air weapon’s classification nor the proof and invoice requirements.

What the exemption does not permit

Licence-exempt status is not permission to carry, display or discharge an airgun anywhere. Property rules, public-safety laws, local orders and transport conditions can still matter. Keep it unloaded and secured in storage, control the muzzle and use a suitable pellet trap and backstop.

Nishana’s home airgun range guide covers backstop planning, while the airgun maintenance guide explains unloaded, dry storage. Those are safety resources, not legal permission to shoot at a particular address. Obtain the property owner’s consent and check local rules first.

Modification is another risk. A power change that takes a model above 20 J can change its licensing category even if it began as a licence-exempt airgun. Do not modify a product to defeat its documented specification, and do not assume the original invoice settles the classification after alteration.

Red flags before buying

  • “All airguns are licence-free.” False; larger-bore or above-threshold air weapons can require licensing.
  • No muzzle-energy specification. FPS alone cannot answer the joule question without projectile mass.
  • No request for buyer proofs. Licence-exempt sales still require identity and residence proof under Rule 84.
  • No invoice with buyer particulars. That conflicts with the stated sale process for the exempt category.
  • Promises that local rules never matter. Location-specific restrictions can exist; verify when in doubt.

Frequently asked questions

Are all airguns licence-exempt in India?

No. The MHA says air weapons exceeding 20 J and/or a bore exceeding .177 inch/4.5 mm are subject to licensing. Nishana's claim is limited to the one configuration it sells: the .177/4.5 mm, approximately 3-joule Star RX Gen 3.

Is every .177 airgun licence-exempt?

No. A .177/4.5 mm model must still remain within the energy limit. If it exceeds 20 J, the energy threshold brings it into the licensed category.

Is every airgun at or below 20 J licence-exempt?

Energy is only one part of the test. A bore exceeding .177 inch/4.5 mm triggers the other side of the MHA’s “and/or” distinction. Nishana's RX Gen 3 configuration stays within both limits.

Why must I provide proof if the airgun is licence-exempt?

Because buyer exemption and dealer obligations are different. Rule 84 requires identity and residence proof and an invoice containing the buyer particulars for sale of the qualifying lower-energy/lower-bore category.

Can a regular marketplace seller skip Form VIII-A?

The MHA FAQ states that licence-exempt category air weapons may be kept and sold only through air-weapon dealers holding Form VIII-A. The sales platform does not replace that dealer requirement.

Do CO₂, spring and PCP airguns have different licence rules?

The threshold distinction discussed here turns on bore and muzzle energy, not the power source. Any mechanism can produce a model on either side of the limits, so check documented specifications.

What if my district has a temporary restriction?

Follow the current local order. Central licence exemption does not cancel a valid location-specific restriction. Contact the relevant licensing authority or local police for an authoritative answer about your area.

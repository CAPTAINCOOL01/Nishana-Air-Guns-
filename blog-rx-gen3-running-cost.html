Ownership guide

RX Gen 3 running cost, made measurable.

Budget 12 g CO₂ and .177 pellets with your own current prices—not a stale rupee estimate—then protect the four magazines, seals and pistol with a simple owner routine.

Answer first

The Star RX Gen 3 running cost is the cost of one .177 pellet plus its share of one 12 g CO₂ cartridge, with a small separate allowance for storage supplies and eventual seal service. There is no honest universal rupee figure because pellet and cartridge prices change, while gas yield changes with temperature, shooting pace and the individual pistol. Use the worksheet below with the prices you can actually buy at today.

The Star RX Gen 3 product page identifies the essentials: a 12 g CO₂ power source, .177 (4.5 mm) pellets and four supplied 8-shot rotary magazines. Those four magazines hold 32 pellets when all are filled; they do not promise 32 shots from a cartridge and they do not change the pistol’s gas consumption.

Use the 50–80-shot range carefully

The current product page lists around 50–80 consistent shots per 12 g cartridge, depending on pace and temperature. Treat that as a planning band tied to the present product-page specification—not a guarantee. Rapid firing cools CO₂; seal condition and the owner’s definition of the final “usable” shot also affect results.

For a first budget, calculate both ends. The optimistic cartridge count is your planned shots divided by 80; the cautious count is the same shots divided by 50. Always round cartridges up to a whole number. Once you have your own records, replace that range with your observed average, Y.

Short sessions deserve extra attention. If you puncture a fresh cartridge but do not use most of its capacity, safe end-of-session handling can make real consumption higher than a simple shots-per-cartridge division suggests. Record actual cartridges opened each month. Never loosen or try to save a pressurised cartridge contrary to the supplied manual.

For a price-free example, suppose you plan 320 shots in a month—ten complete four-magazine sets. The product-page band implies roughly four cartridges at 80 shots each or seven cartridges after rounding up at 50. Multiply that 4–7 range by the cartridge price available to you, then add 320 times your pellet unit price. No invented market price is needed.

Four magazines: convenience, care and cost control

The supplied four 8-shot rotary magazines let you stage 32 pellets before a string. Fill each chamber with the correct .177 pellet in the orientation shown by the manual, and make sure skirts do not protrude. Never force a damaged pellet or reluctant magazine. If one magazine repeatedly feeds poorly while the others do not, stop using it and seek model-specific advice.

Count magazine cycles to forecast pellets: one magazine equals up to eight pellets; one complete four-magazine set equals up to 32. This is also an easy way to spot waste from damaged pellets or casual dry firing. Avoid dry firing unless the manufacturer’s instructions expressly permit it.

Storage and seals: prevent avoidable expense

At the end of a session, point in a safe direction, remove the magazine, verify the chamber is clear and follow the manual’s procedure for the CO₂ system. Do not store the pistol pressurised for long periods. Equally, do not improvise removal of a cartridge that may still contain pressure. The manual and the warning printed on the cylinder take priority over generic internet advice.

Store the unloaded pistol in a dry, locked place inaccessible to unauthorised users and children. Keep cartridges away from direct sun, hot vehicles, flames and other heat sources; never puncture or incinerate them. A fresh desiccant pouch can help in humid storage, but it does not replace periodically checking the case and wiping moisture or fingerprints from exterior metal.

Seals are wear components, but there is no responsible fixed “replace every X months” rule for every climate and usage pattern. Persistent hissing, visible gas escape or a repeatable unexplained fall in cartridge yield are reasons to stop, unload safely and ask for service guidance. Use only manufacturer-approved seal lubricant, in the amount and location the manual specifies. Petroleum products, excess oil and casual disassembly can create new faults.

Safe maintenance boundary: routine ownership means inspection, a dry exterior wipe, careful magazine cleaning and the manual’s approved lubrication steps. Valve work, seal replacement or disassembly beyond the owner manual belongs with a qualified service professional. For a broader routine, read Nishana’s airgun maintenance and storage guide.

A simple ownership rhythm

  1. Before shooting: inspect the muzzle, magazine and visible seals; confirm the correct .177 pellets and an undamaged 12 g cartridge.
  2. During shooting: use a safe backstop and eye protection, keep the muzzle controlled, and pause if sound, gas leakage or pellet impact changes unexpectedly.
  3. After shooting: unload, count magazines, handle remaining pressure exactly as the manual directs, wipe the exterior and return the pistol to dry locked storage.
  4. Monthly: update shots, cartridges and pellet purchases. Your own average is more useful than someone else’s one-session claim.

Frequently asked questions

What is the simplest RX Gen 3 cost-per-shot formula?

Add the current price of one .177 pellet to the current price of one 12 g cartridge divided by your observed usable shots from that cartridge: P + (C ÷ Y). Keep the pistol purchase and optional accessories separate.

Is 50–80 shots per cartridge guaranteed?

No. It is the range stated on the current Nishana product page and is explicitly variable with pace and temperature. Use it for first-pass planning, then budget from your own recorded results.

Do the four magazines increase CO₂ use?

Not by themselves. They hold up to 32 pellets in total and reduce reloading pauses. Shooting faster can cool a CO₂ cartridge, however, so pace may affect the usable yield you observe.

Can I leave a partly used cartridge installed?

Avoid long-term pressurised storage. For any partly used cartridge, follow the exact supplied-manual procedure; do not loosen, remove or modify a pressurised cylinder based on generic advice.

Which pellets belong in the RX Gen 3?

Use quality .177 (4.5 mm) pellets that meet the product manual’s requirements. Do not substitute BBs, darts, damaged pellets or another calibre unless the manufacturer expressly approves them for this model.

Should I buy seals on a calendar schedule?

No universal interval applies. Keep a reasonable care reserve, monitor for leakage or a repeatable fall in yield, and obtain the exact model-specific part and service advice when symptoms appear.
